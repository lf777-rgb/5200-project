Tehran Food Prices | 2025-2026

When Staples Slip Away

An interactive data story on how rice, wheat flour, and key food categories surged in Tehran and across Iran from March 2025 to March 2026.

Core question

How sharply did staple food prices rise in Tehran, and how much faster did food costs move than the rest of everyday life?

The story follows local market evidence for staples, then widens to the food categories that shaped household affordability.

Opening

A basic basket became a moving target

Food inflation can sound abstract when it appears as a single national percentage. In Tehran, the sharper story begins with staples that households encounter directly: rice, wheat flour, cooking oil, dairy, meat, and produce. When those items move faster than the wider economy, the effect is felt not as a statistic but as a shrinking set of daily choices.

Story focus

This project follows two linked threads: Tehran market evidence for rice and wheat flour, and official food-category inflation snapshots that show how widely the pressure spread across the household food basket.

Narrative arc

Start local, widen outward, return to households

  • Opening: anchor the reader in an ordinary Tehran shopping trip.
  • Middle 1: show what happened to rice and wheat flour in the Tehran market.
  • Middle 2: show which national food categories saw the fastest inflation.
  • Middle 3: translate category shocks into household pressure.
  • Ending: explain what became difficult to afford first, and why that matters.

Method note

How to read this simulator

Each preset assigns weights to five recurring food groups. A March 2025 basket is treated as an index value of 100. The 2026 basket is then estimated using the documented year-over-year changes for those groups.

For that reason, the simulator is labeled as an illustrative affordability aid rather than a precise household expenditure survey. Its purpose is to make the scale of category inflation easier to compare across different food priorities.

Takeaway

Technical Appendix

Methods, data notes, and limitations

This appendix documents how the visual evidence was assembled and how each chart should be interpreted. The main article is written for a general audience; this section is for readers who want the data logic, assumptions, and caveats.

Data design

This project combines Tehran market evidence from FAO/GIEWS with published English-language summaries of official Iranian inflation data that cite the Statistical Center of Iran.

Geographic scope

Local market evidence is Tehran-specific. Food-category inflation snapshots are national context, so the project keeps local price evidence and national category evidence visually distinct.

Chart logic

Static Figure 1 uses documented year-over-year checkpoints. Interactive Graphic 2 converts category inflation rates into a weighted index using a March 2025 baseline of 100.

Limitations

Several figures are based on source summaries such as "about four times higher" or "more than triple." Those values should be treated as anchored approximations until the underlying monthly series is downloaded.

Reproducibility note

The charts are generated in JavaScript from values documented in the source files and source list. A fuller monthly FPMA export would improve the time-series resolution but is not required for the current chart logic.

Audience note

The main narrative is written for the general public. This appendix records caveats, proxies, and data decisions for a more technical reader.

Basket index formula

The household basket simulator treats March 2025 as an index of 100 and applies weighted year-over-year food-category increases.

basket index = 100 + sum(category weight x category YoY inflation)

The preset basket weights are illustrative. They are designed for comparison, not as a claim about the exact spending pattern of a specific Tehran household.
